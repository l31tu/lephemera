Manuia le vaiaso o le Gagana Sāmoa!

’Afa’afakasi is an ongoing project attempting to reconnect to Gagana Sāmoa (Samoan language) and aganu'u (culture). This issue is called Folasaga Lona Lua (Second Introduction) and will focus on basic words and phrases.

Lominga Tasi (Issue One) has been created in celebration of Vaiaso o le Gagana Sāmoa (Samoan Language Week) which runs from Sunday 29 May to Saturday 4 June, 2022.

Gagana Sāmoa

Gagana Sāmoa is one of the five Samoic languages (the others are Tuvaluan, Tokelauan, Pukapukan and Niuafo'ou). It is also similar to other languages of the pacific such as te reo Māori, lea faka-Tonga, vagahau Niue, 'ōlelo Hawai'i, and te reo Kuki Airani (Cook Islands Māori).

There are three levels of Gagana Sāmoa. Everyday gagana Sāmoa is used with friends, family and acquaintances. Formal gagana is used when talking to strangers and people of status. Formal gagana fa'amatai is the language used by chiefs.

Tālofa, tōfā

Tālofa (hello) is pronounced 'tah-loh-fah' or sometimes 'kah-loh-fah' when pronounced casually. Mālō, pronounced 'mah-loh', can also be used to say hello. To show respect tālōfa lava can be used.

Tōfā (goodbye) pronounced 'toh-fah', or simply fā are used in everyday situations. Tōfā soifūa is used in more formal circumstances.

Fa'afetai

This project was conceived and implemented on the unceded lands of the Boon Wurrung and Wurundjeri people. It also features contributions from people living on Yugambeh Country (Gold Coast) and in Aotearoa (New Zealand).

Contributers to this issue are all aiga and their family village, Faleasi’u, is located in Upolu, Samoa. They have varying degrees of knowledge and experience with Samoan language and culture.

This is an ongoing learning process, so it is likely that mistakes will be made. If you see information that is incorrect or would like to give feedback please email .

This project is a part of Le Phem Era, a creative research, archival and publishing practice based in Naarm (Melbourne, Australia).

La'u sootaga

'Afa'afakasi, essentially meaning half of a half Samoan, is a play on the term ‘Afakasi (half Samoan). 'Afa'afakasi was created by Leitu Bonnici, who is a quarter Samoan but was born and raised in Australia and is mostly of European ethnicity.

She is both an insider and an outsider, being mostly removed from Samoan and Australian Samoan culture. Her knowledge of the culture is through interactions with family, second hand accounts and as a tourist in Samoa.
