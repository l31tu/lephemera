Manuia le vaiaso o le Gagana Sāmoa!

’Afa’afakasi is an ongoing project attempting to reconnect to Gagana Sāmoa (Samoan language) and aganu'u (culture). This issue is called Folasaga Lona Lua (Second Introduction) and will focus on basic words and phrases.

Lominga Tasi (Issue One) has been created in celebration of Vaiaso o le Gagana Sāmoa (Samoan Language Week) which runs from Sunday 29 May to Saturday 4 June, 2022.

Gagana Sāmoa

Gagana Sāmoa is one of the five Samoic languages (the others are Tuvaluan, Tokelauan, Pukapukan and Niuafo'ou). It is also similar to other languages of the pacific such as te reo Māori, lea faka-Tonga, vagahau Niue, 'ōlelo Hawai'i, and te reo Kuki Airani (Cook Islands Māori).

There are three levels of Gagana Sāmoa. Everyday gagana is used with friends, family and acquaintances. Formal gagana is used when talking to strangers and people of status. Formal gagana fa'amatai is the language used by chiefs.

Tālofa ma tōfā

Tālōfa lava (hello) is the respectful way to say greet someone and is pronounced 'tah-loh-fah lah-vah'. Tālōfa is often used on its own and is sometimes pronounced 'kah-loh-fah' when said casually. Mālō, pronounced 'mah-loh', can also be used to say hello.

Tōfā soifūa (goodbye), pronounced 'toh-fah soy-foo-ah', is used in formal circumstances. Tōfā, or simply fā, are used in everyday situations.

Pronunciation

Gagana Sāmoa is a phonetic language, meaning words are usually pronounced as they are written. Konesona (consonants) are always followed by a vaueli (vowel) and every vaueli in a word is sounded out. There are no silent letters, consonant clusters or blends. Both short vaueli sounds and long vaueli sounds are used, for example 'A' and 'Ā'.

There are fifteen original letters in the Samoan alphabet including the ('), koma liliu or ʻokina (glottal stop), as well as three borrowed letters from the English alphabet (H, K and R). All the konesona have the same sounds as in the English alphabet with the exception of ‘G’, which is pronounced as ‘NG'. Also note that 'P' is pronounced closer to 'B' and that 'T' is often replaced with a 'K' sound when spoken casually.

Le tau

Sāmoa is a tropical and does not have four distinct seasons. The weather is generally differentiated into tau mātūtū (dry season) between May and October, and tau o tīmuga (wet season) between November and April.

Lā'ei

Lā'ei (clothing) is the formal word for clothes and is pronounced 'lah-??'. However, generally clothing is referred to as lāvalava or 'ofu, pronounced 'oh-foo'. The 'ie lāvalava, pronounced 'ee-eh la-vah-la-vah', is a wrap around rectangular printed cloth and is daily clothing item in Sāmoa.

In ancient times, clothing was made from trees such as the coconut palm and the paper mulberry. Traditional ceremonial costumes and accessories are still worn today. When linen, cotton and silk arrived in Sāmoa in the 1830s, a distinctive Samoan style of clothing emerged. The puletasi, pronounced 'poo-leh-tah-see', is a classic two piece dress worn by women and fa'afafine (third gender). Men and fa'afatama (fourth gender) wear a 'iefaitaga, pronounced 'ee-eh-fai-tah-ngah'.

Mea'ai

'Ai means to eat. Mea'ai is food. Tāpena means to prepare. Tāpenaga means preparation. Taro Umu (earth oven)

Food is an important aspect of fa'asāmoa. When entertaining guests, it is important to make sure that there is enough food for everyone and some left over for guests to take home. Traditionally just the men prepared food, but nowadays it is usually a shared responsibility amongst the family.

In many Samoan families, there are two main meals a day with the main one in the evening when all members of the family are at home. Toanai is a traditional family buffet?

Fa'afetai

This project was created on the unceded lands of the Boon Wurrung and Wurundjeri people. It also features contributions from people born in Samoa but living on Yugambeh Country (Gold Coast) and in Aotearoa (New Zealand).

Contributers to this issue are all aiga and their family village, Faleāsi’u, is located in 'Upolu, Samoa. They have varying degrees of knowledge and experience with Samoan language and culture.

This is an ongoing learning process, so it is likely that mistakes will be made. If you see information that is incorrect or would like to give feedback please email .

This project is a part of Le Phem Era, a creative research, archival and publishing practice based in Naarm (Melbourne, Australia).

La'u sootaga

'Afa'afakasi, essentially meaning half of a half Samoan, is a play on the term ‘Afakasi (half Samoan). 'Afa'afakasi was created by Leitu Bonnici, who is a quarter Samoan but was born and raised in Australia and is mostly of European ethnicity. She is mostly removed from Samoan and Australian Samoan culture. Her knowledge of the culture is through interactions with family, second hand accounts and as a tourist in Samoa.
